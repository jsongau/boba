While you're here

How tea became boba: the short history

You found a missing page; here's how a missing-page story usually goes in tea. Every era below started with someone pouring something nobody asked for yet.

The legend

A leaf falls into hot water

Chinese legend credits Emperor Shennong, nearly five thousand years ago: leaves drifted into his boiling water, he drank it anyway, and preferred it. A story, not a record — but every cup since has kept its side of the bargain.

8th century

Tea gets its book

In Tang-dynasty China, Lu Yu wrote the Cha Jing — the Classic of Tea — and turned a drink into a discipline: which water, which fire, which cup. Tea houses spread along the trade roads, and drinking tea together became its own reason to gather.

1980s Taiwan

Someone drops pearls in

In the 1980s, tapioca pearls met cold milk tea in Taiwan — and two tearooms, Chun Shui Tang in Taichung and Hanlin in Tainan, still both claim the first cup. The argument was never settled. The drink didn't wait for a verdict.

1990s California

The pearls cross the Pacific

Taiwanese families brought boba to Southern California, and the San Gabriel Valley became its American home — tea houses open past midnight, full of students, first dates, and the debrief after dinner. The 626 has poured it nightly ever since.

The densest tea corridor in the country

SoCal now holds 998 boba shops across 169 cities, from Ventura to San Diego — brown sugar strips, fruit-tea houses, 2 AM counters. This site keeps their hours checked so your night doesn't end at a locked door. Or a missing page.
